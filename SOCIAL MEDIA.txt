                                                                                        SOCIAL MEDIA

                                                                                       DO'S AND DON'TS

Keeping your digital footprint clean is a bit like maintaining a professional portfolio; it's always better to be proactive than to have to scrub your history later.



The "Do's" of Responsible Social Media
Audit Your Privacy Settings: Regularly check your "View As" profile settings to ensure you know exactly what the public (and potential recruiters) can see.

Showcase Your Projects: Use platforms like LinkedIn or GitHub to share your technical wins, whether it’s a coding challenge you completed or a research report you're proud of.

Network with Intent: Follow industry leaders and organizations in your field to stay updated on trends and internship opportunities.

Think Before You Post: Apply the "24-hour rule" for heated topics—if you’re feeling emotional, wait a day before posting to ensure your response remains objective and professional.

Verify Your Sources: Before sharing a link or a "fact," take a moment to cross-reference it. Being a source of reliable information builds your personal brand as a detail-oriented student.



The "Don'ts" of Responsible Social Media
Don’t Overshare Personal Data: Avoid posting photos that inadvertently show your ID, home address, or real-time location tags that could compromise your safety.

Don’t Engage in Online Conflicts: Avoid "flame wars" or aggressive arguments; digital screenshots last much longer than the heat of the moment.

Don’t Post Unprofessional Content: Steer clear of sharing photos or text that depict illegal activities, academic dishonesty, or behavior that contradicts a professional environment.

Don’t Neglect Your "Tagged" Photos: Don't forget that others can tag you in content. Regularly review your tagged photos and remove your name from anything that doesn't align with your image.

Don’t Plagiarize Content: Never share someone else's work, charts, or code as your own. Always tag the original creator or provide a source link to maintain academic and professional integrity.
